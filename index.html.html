Circulatory System

  • Transporting Oxygen: It picks up oxygen from your lungs and delivers it to every cell.

  • Delivering Nutrients: It carries nutrients from your digestive system to where they're needed.

  • Removing Waste: It takes away waste products (like carbon dioxide) from your cells to be eliminated by your body.

  • Fighting Infections: It carries immune cells to fight off germs.

  • Regulating Body Temperature: It helps distribute heat throughout your body.

1. The Heart: This is the muscular pump that keeps blood moving.

  •  It has four chambers that work together to pump blood to the lungs and the rest of the body.

2. Blood Vessels: These are the tubes that blood travels through.

  • Arteries: Carry blood away from the heart. They usually carry oxygenated blood (except for the pulmonary artery).
  • Veins: Carry blood towards the heart. They usually carry deoxygenated blood (except for the pulmonary veins).
  • Capillaries: These are tiny, thin vessels where the exchange of oxygen, nutrients, and waste happens between the blood and your cells.

3. Blood: This is the fluid that carries everything. It's made up of:

  • Red blood cells: Carry oxygen.
  • White blood cells: Fight infection.
  • Platelets: Help with clotting.
  • Plasma: The liquid part that carries cells, nutrients, and waste.

The Heart: Parts and Functions

(The heart is a four-chambered muscle.)

  • Atria (Upper Chambers):
  • Right Atrium: Receives deoxygenated blood from the body.

  • Left Atrium: Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs.

Ventricles (Lower Chambers):

  • Right Ventricle: Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs.
  • Left Ventricle: Pumps oxygenated blood to the rest of the body. This is the strongest chamber because it has the longest distance to pump blood.

Blood Flow Pathway

1.Deoxygenated blood from the body enters the Right Atrium.

2.It then flows through a valve into the Right Ventricle.

3.The Right Ventricle pumps this blood to the lungs through the pulmonary artery.

4.In the lungs, the blood releases carbon dioxide and picks up oxygen.

5.Oxygenated blood returns from the  lungs to the Left Atrium through the pulmonary veins.

6.It then flows through a valve into the Left Ventricle.

7.The Left Ventricle pumps this oxygen-rich blood out to the rest of the body through the aorta (the largest artery).

8.The blood travels through arteries, then smaller vessels called capillaries, delivering oxygen and nutrients to your cells.

9.After dropping off oxygen and picking up waste, the deoxygenated blood returns to the Right Atrium via veins.

Blood Circulation

  • Pulmonary Circulation
  •  This is the path blood takes between your heart and your lungs.

  • Your heart pumps deoxygenated blood to your lungs.In the lungs, the blood picks up oxygen and drops off carbon dioxide.

  • Then, oxygenated blood returns to your heart.

  • Systemic Circulation
  • This is the path blood takes between your heart and the rest of your body.

  • Your heart pumps oxygenated blood to all your organs and tissues.

  • Your cells use the oxygen and nutrients, and pick up waste products like carbon dioxide.

  • Deoxygenated blood then returns to your heart.

Topic 2 Immune System 

Immune System Overview

  1. Definition of immune system
  •  The immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs that work together to defend the body against harmful invaders like bacteria, viruses, and other pathogens.

  1. Main function

The main function of the immune system is to protect your body from harmful invaders like bacteria, viruses, and other germs.

  • Recognizing these foreign substances (called antigens)
  • Attacking and destroying them.
  • Remembering them so it can fight them off faster if they come back.

Lines of Defense (Immune System)

  •  1st Physical & Chemical Barriers (First line)

These stop germs form entering your body.

  • Physical: skin, mucus, cilia in the respiratory tract
  • Chemical: stomach acid, sweat/oil (enzymes, salt, low pH), antimicrobial substances in tears and saliva.

  • 2nd Inflammatory Response (Second line)

If germs get past the barriers, the body reacts by sending immune cells to the infected area.

  • Blood vessels dilate amd become more ‘‘leaky’’
  • Immune cells (like phagocytes) move out of blood to the tissue
  • This causes redness, heat, swelling, and pain

  • Specific Immunity (Third line)

This is the targeted defense against a specific pathogen and creates memory

  • Antigens (foreign markers on pathogens)
  • Antibodies (made by B cells) that bind to specific pathogens
  • T cells that help destroy infected cells and coordinate the response

Specific immunity

The immune response that targets particular antigens and forms memory cells for faster, stronger reactions.

White Blood Cells

  • Neutrophils
  • They are a type of white blood cell that engulf and destroy bacteria and fungi.

They're usually the first ones to arrive at the site of an infection.

  • Macrophages
  • They also engulf and digest pathogens, dead cells, and debris.

They can also signal to other immune cells, helping to coordinate the attack.

Lymphocytes

  • B Cells:
  • Make antibodies that attach to specific pathogens.
  • These antibodies flag the pathogens for destruction or neutralize them directly.
  • Some B cells become memory cells for long-term immunity.
  • T Cells:
  • Helper T Cells:

Coordinate the immune response, activating other immune cells like B cells and killer T cells.

  • Killer T Cells (Cytotoxic T Lymphocytes):

Directly kill infected body cells or cancer cells.

Some T cells also become memory cells.
